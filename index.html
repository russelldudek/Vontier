The real mandate

Make distributed innovation behave like one enterprise capability.

Vontier's portfolio spans payments, point-of-sale, remote diagnostics, site automation, EV charging, fleet telematics, industrial controls, and repair workflows. The role must preserve business-unit speed while creating enough common architecture, evidence, governance, and learning to scale.

01

Autonomy vs. common leverage

Business units need room to solve local customer problems; the enterprise needs reusable patterns, shared guardrails, and transparent investment choices.

02

Pilot speed vs. production truth

Fast experiments are valuable only when evidence, ownership, data quality, controls, deployment, monitoring, and adoption are designed from the start.

03

Cloud intelligence vs. edge reality

AI must work with PLCs, sensors, payment systems, devices, technicians, bandwidth constraints, safety, cyber controls, and installed-base economics.

04

Outside-in innovation vs. VBS discipline

The company must absorb ecosystem advances rapidly without replacing kaizen, operating ownership, and measurable customer outcomes with novelty theater.

Innovation Torque Converter

Three modes. One governed transmission.

The operating model is not a stage-gate bureaucracy. It is a controlled coupling mechanism: allow productive slip while uncertainty is high, then lock the system when evidence and ownership are strong enough to transmit value at scale.

Design hypothesis: Vontier's strongest enterprise advantage may come from a common evidence and deployment contract - not a common list of AI tools.
O

Open: harvest without commitment

Capture ideas from customers, operators, products, partners, field service, support, and market intelligence. Make the problem, decision, user, economics, and data reality visible before choosing technology.

C

Coupling: prove under controlled slip

Run fast pilots with explicit success evidence, human authority, model/data controls, ownership, deployment assumptions, and a stop condition. Learn cheaply without pretending the solution is production-ready.

L

Lock-up: transmit and compound

Move proven work into production with reusable architecture, MLOps, security, validation, adoption, monitoring, economics, and VBS review. Return the learning to every business unit that can use it.

First 120 days

Earn the right to standardize.

The first move should not be an enterprise-wide tool rollout. It should be a rapid operating diagnosis, an explicit portfolio contract, two or three production-shaped proofs, and a visible mechanism that business units find useful enough to adopt.

Days 0-30

Read the transmission

Map strategic priorities, customer signals, active pilots, architectures, data dependencies, IT/OT constraints, vendor exposure, adoption burden, and business-unit ownership.

Days 31-60

Define the contract

Install the common idea brief, evidence requirements, human-authority rules, architecture patterns, MLOps/security gates, VBS review cadence, and stop/scale decisions.

Days 61-90

Prove transmission

Run a small portfolio of production-shaped pilots spanning an operational workflow, a customer-facing opportunity, and an edge/industrial use case. Instrument value and adoption from day one.

Days 91-120

Lock and compound

Move the strongest proof toward production, publish reusable assets, establish the virtual expert network, and show the executive team a transparent portfolio of scale, continue, hold, and stop decisions.
